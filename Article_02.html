Breaking Down Barriers: Discussing
Autism and Community Building

07/12/2024 | Kimiko Yanagisawa

On my daily walk around the block of my neighborhood I feel the pressure of my thoughts against the skull that holds my mind. When stuck inside writing I feel pushed to the brink of insanity and find a sense of relief in spending some moments in the nature that surrounds my home.

Its normalcy, suburban bliss, like a tether, brings me back down to earth. As if the simplicity of life here invokes a resounding sense of calmness that reigns in my existential thoughts. My neighborhood, albeit quiet, seems to still always be bustling. Groups of chatty schoolgirls walk home with one another. Their laughter and joy brings a sense of life into the streets of my neighborhood that I find refreshing. Couples and friends join one another in strolls, often with animal companions in stride. At times it feels as though my walk is an observation of individuals partaking in communion with one another rather than a mission to exercise the body and release the mind.

At one point during my walk, the voice of a past love rolls into my mind. Her voice, in a whisper, conveys that she could never live in an area so domestic. Her words meet my ears yet again and just as they did when she first spoke them, they strike me as odd. What is so bad about living in the suburbs anyways? I don’t entertain the thought for long as something out of place catches my eye mid stroll. I stop, caught in my tracks, and stare down at the bush below me. At knee level is a book, perfectly vacuum sealed in plastic, resting atop the leaves. It looks pristine, held almost as a gift, reaching up towards me. I am tempted to peek into the book without any commitment towards keeping it, but the plastic protecting it prevents me from doing just that.

The words speak out to me clearly: ‘Authoring Autism / on rhetoric and neurological queerness.’ I had never thought of Autism, let alone its ideas pertaining to rhetoric and neurological queerness. Committed now, spurred by curiosity, I pick up my new book and rip off the plastic, a divine feeling flowing.

Shocked by its contents and shamed by such truth that I soon realize the book itself portrays ideologies and concerns that not only had I never considered, but relayed truths that overlapped to every other minority group I had studied at university. Of course, autistic people would feel this way, but how had I never considered them? How had I never considered how their truths could teach me lessons, not only of their own community, but of my own?

The more I read the more I began to understand that it is much easier to dehumanize those with traits that threaten prosocial norms than it is to make room, adjust for and empathize with those individuals. To be exclusive not only evokes a sense of power over others, but it is much easier than the practice of inclusion.

Melanie Yergeau, author of Authoring Autism, not only depicts the struggles many autistic persons face when kept in the shadows but additionally relays a vulnerable truth that her own experience with autism has left her with moments of uncontrollable bodily movements, being spoken for as opposed to spoken with and exiled. Yergeau’s divine evocation of feeling, thought and truth proves as a direct opposition to countless preconceived notions surrounding autism.

In its most simple form, Yergeau’s sentiments convey a feeling that resides in all of us. A feeling of being deeply misunderstood, fear of rejection and exclusion, to not only feel out of control of the body, but to be a slave to its whims. To feel alone and to be left alone intentionally is a feeling I hope we can all spare one another. Residing in each of us is the power to include, to bring one another closer together through understanding and maybe in doing so we will reach another level of heightened thought and enlightened perspective.
